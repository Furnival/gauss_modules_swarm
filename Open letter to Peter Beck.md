Of course, every dad remembers the moment when his dad passed. Perhaps he has a new child. Perhaps he realizes that his responsibilities have grown and that he is responsible for a larger family now - not just his nuclear family but the wider family.  He is perhaps not the oldest, but he is now the most senior in the family. Peter Beck is not, anymore, the young kid who enthusiastically visited the USA to see and to be disappointed by what NASA had built since Apollo. This is, in the serious sense, the senior moment for Peter Beck. He is not the oldest or the richest, but he is the most serious and most stable. I think responsibility has fallen to him. And not just for his company, Rocket Lab.
In the summer of 1964 my family drove down to Wallops Island for the National Association of Rocketry’s annual meet, a long weekend organized around a hobby that I was probably more serious about than any of the adults who brought me there. NASA had set up a display for the visiting model rocketeers, and the part I remember was meant for boys my age. A real device sat on a table with no glass around it, no rope line, no museum distance, just an actual piece of hardware that a NASA engineer let us walk up to and inspect from too close. He explained that it ran on electricity instead of chemical fuel, that it would one day push spacecraft to the planets, and that the thrust it produced was roughly the weight of a paperclip. I remember the shock of that comparison, the absurdity of it, and the possibility that this tiny, almost ridiculous machine might belong to the same future as the rockets I was building in my head. I was twelve years old and could not decide whether I was being teased or admitted, very briefly, into the machinery of the world.
For people of my age, Mercury and Gemini and Apollo were not simply programs. They were part of  childhood’s weather. It colored classrooms, magazine covers, toy stores, newspaper diagrams, dinner-table conversation, and the private architecture of ambition. Even the adults, who we called “dults,” who, fantastically, could not have explained orbital rendezvous or ablative heat shields understood that something technically serious and publicly important was underway. Engineers, test pilots, contractors, machinists, mathematicians, and men in white shirts with narrow ties seemed to be moving the boundary of the possible in full view of the country. The machines were real, but so was the atmosphere around them. It suggested that precision, nerve, calculation, and public purpose could still converge on something clean.
I do not mean to overly sentimentalize that period - but of course I am. The country carried Vietnam inside it, and the Cold War, and racial brutality, and all the confusions that people my age have no business pretending not to remember. But it also carried a live expectation that the future could be made, and made in public, by difficult and disciplined work. That expectation mattered more than we understood at the time. It gave shape to childhood. It made technical life feel morally legible. It suggested that a society could ask hard things of itself for reasons not wholly reducible to appetite, fear, or accounting.
What makes the present feel so different is not simply that the politics are uglier, though they are, or that war has returned to the center of daily consciousness, though it has. It is that the horizon seems lower. Public life feels full of transaction, scandal, tactical accusation, and the continual management of emergency. The day’s language arrives as market reaction, electoral theater, battlefield footage, and one more reason to narrow one’s expectations. The feeling is not, to me, like the front edge of the 1960s. It is darker and less generous than that and 30 years earlier. I do not mean that history is repeating itself in any literal way. I mean only that the emotional weather feels less like ascent than like prelude, as if the culture has become less good at absorbing shocks and poorer at imagining destinations.
In a time like that, dreams begin to seem ornamental, which is one reason they become so easy to dismiss. But I know they are not ornamental at all. They are structural. Societies are held together partly by the scale of the future they permit themselves to believe in. Remove that scale and a great many things continue—the markets open, the armies move, budgets get passed, quarterly reports are filed—but the inner rationale of effort begins to thin out. People still work. They still raise children and buy houses and walk the dog and depending on who they are, pay taxes. But the work takes place under a lower ceiling. One begins to live in middle management.
This is one reason I keep thinking about Peter Beck. He belongs, as I do, to the afterglow of that earlier period, though at the trailing edge of it rather than the center. He is not, by temperament, a dream merchant. This is one reason I admire him. He does not present himself as a prophet and he does not seem eager to run a public company as a personality cult. He counts the pennies. He wants every project to close. He behaves like a man who knows that rockets are unforgiving and balance sheets are also unforgiving, and that a public company can be killed by theater as surely as by bad engineering. In a time when so much public language has become inflated, there is something bracing about that.
And yet I wonder whether caution, under present conditions, can become too complete a reading of responsibility. A chief executive owes shareholders discipline, certainly. He owes them an honest account of risk, cost, schedule, and return. But in a company that builds rockets—and especially in one followed by a retail public that does not seem entirely cynical—I suspect he may owe them something else as well. Not fantasy. Not lies. Not one more messianic promise detached from execution. Something more difficult: a future large enough to deserve their patience.
I think of the people who follow Rocket Lab now in places that did not exist when I was twelve: on YouTube, in investor channels, in the long argument threads on Reddit, in the peculiar half-public neighborhoods where a company’s retail holders gather to talk among themselves. They are not abstractions. They have jobs, spouses, children, mortgages, anxieties, brokerage accounts open on kitchen tables. Some are studying margins and launch cadence. Some are trying to decide whether patience is conviction or delusion. Some, no doubt, are simply trying to catch a good trade. But not all of them are. There is something else mixed in. You can feel it in the attention they pay, in the tolerance some of them show for delay or dilution, if delay or dilution appears attached to a project still large enough to matter.
One of the reasons I trust this feeling is that it appears in such ordinary forms. Dave G, on YouTube, will spend a long stretch discussing backlog, launch cadence, capital allocation, margins, and Beck’s temperament, and then mention more or less in passing that he took some gains out for a house and a car when he had to move, while keeping most of the rest invested. That detail matters to me. It has rooms in it, payments, a growing family, a life taking shape in the old terrestrial way while some portion of belief remains committed to a rocket company. It is neither zealotry nor detachment.
My wife is a doctor in the ICU. She works, of course, to make money, but no sane person would say that money is what her work is finally for. She works to save lives. A rocket company is not a hospital, and shareholders are not waiting rooms full of the afflicted. Still, the comparison seems useful to me. Serious work can be both financially disciplined and ordered toward something larger than revenue extraction. I respect Beck’s conservatism for that reason. He is trying to keep the enterprise real. But I also wonder whether part of his responsibility to his shareholders includes the dreams they have for their children, or perhaps only the permission not to give those dreams up too early.
That question had been in the back of my mind when I started thinking about Rocket Lab’s Hall thrusters, and then its completely integrated and mass produced Gauss units, not as individual spacecraft components but as industrial objects—small propulsion modules that might one day be made in the thousands. I had recently written about them as a kind of swarm technology, “limpets” of a new orbital economy, and I found myself asking Perplexity what out-of-the-box use might emerge if such units ever became cheap enough, numerous enough, and ordinary enough to be treated, less like jewels, than like fittings with a catalogue number. I had in mind geostationary logistics, perhaps, or the long-missing infrastructure between low Earth orbit and GEO. Perplexity suggested a swarm. That seemed sensible. Then it suggested something stranger: not Earth, but Venus; not landers, but air-breathing satellites feeding on the upper atmosphere of a planet we have spent sixty years describing as hostile to hardware. I had gone looking for a market and the Perplexity handed me a planet. Next essay is on Venus.
I think Venus has been on “the back burner”  long enough.
Just my opinion. -- Lawrence Furnival

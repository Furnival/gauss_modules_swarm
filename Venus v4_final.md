Venus, not Earth: Air-breathing satellites will fly first over Venus
The planet with the hellish atmosphere has the best atmosphere as fuel.

A Hall-effect thruster does one thing well. It takes a small amount of inert gas, ionizes it, and accelerates it to about twenty kilometers per second. Give it electricity and propellant and it will run until its nozzle physically erodes. Generally, the catch has been the propellant. Xenon, the first choice, is rare and expensive. It is a byproduct of industrial air separation and priced like a precious metal. Krypton is only a bit better. Argon is cheap but must be stored as a deep cryogen, which wipes out its cost advantage. For any mission longer than a few years, the propellant budget, not the thruster, is the limiting factor.
A dream since the early 1960s is a thruster that does not carry its propellant at all. It scoops it from the atmosphere, ionizes whatever happens to be passing by, and throws it out the back as exhaust. An air-breathing satellite. The spacecraft never runs out of gas because the planet is the gas tank. That dream has been pursued at Earth for fifteen years by ESA, by BUSEK, by a handful of academic groups in Italy and Japan. And it has not quite been realized. The reason it has not been realized is that Earth's upper atmosphere, at the altitudes where this would work, is made of the wrong stuff.
Venus's, by a quiet accident of planetary chemistry, is made of nearly the right stuff. And that is why the first operational air-breathing satellite — the first spacecraft that does not carry its own fuel — is going to fly at the planet that we have spent sixty years describing as hostile to hardware.

I. The atmospheric mismatch — and why it favors Venus
The technology the engineer at Wallops showed our group of amateur rocket enthusiasts in 1964 — the device my previous open letter describes — is finally about to do the thing he promised. The surprise is where.
Air-breathing electric propulsion has been almost-working at Earth for more than fifteen years. The reason it has not yet worked is that Earth's upper atmosphere is made of the wrong stuff. Venus's, by a quiet accident of planetary chemistry, is made of nearly the right stuff. Four differences carry the argument.
Composition. At the altitudes where atmospheric density is high enough to feed a thruster but low enough that drag does not eat the thrust budget — roughly 200 to 250 kilometers at Earth, and 150 to 200 kilometers at Venus — the gases involved are radically different.

The chart tells the whole story in one glance. Earth's thermosphere is dominated by atomic oxygen, one of the more chemically aggressive species a spacecraft can encounter. Atomic O does not merely oxidize the surfaces it strikes; it erodes polymers, embrittles metals, and scours the inside of any discharge channel yet built. The first RAM-EP firing at ESA in 2018 was a milestone, but a narrower one than it is often reported to be: the test used a nitrogen–oxygen molecular mixture from a particle generator simulating 200 km Earth altitude, and net positive thrust was not achieved — drag exceeded thrust by roughly a factor of four. The principle was demonstrated. The economics were not. To this day, no air-breathing electric thruster has produced net positive thrust in any test, on any propellant, at any simulated altitude.
This is the inheritance the Venus case has to improve on. The Mars community has been working the same problem — TransMIT, IRS Stuttgart, and a NASA Phase I in 2016 all targeted Mars CO₂ as the ABEP propellant of choice. The Venus case is closer in chemistry to Mars than to Earth, but distinct from both: denser atmosphere than Mars at scooping altitudes, double the solar flux, and no magnetic field like earth’s to fight.
Venus at the same altitude band is mostly carbon dioxide. CO₂ is chemically dull. It ionizes cleanly, accelerates predictably, and does not eat the hardware that handles it. A 2025 study at Georgia Tech (Moskovitz, Brabston, Lev, and Walker; IEPC-2025-559) ran a sub-500-watt Hall thruster on CO₂ and other molecular propellants and found no oxidation or carbon soot buildup after ten hours of operation. Ten hours is encouraging; it is not the ten thousand the architecture eventually needs. Specific impulse drops compared to xenon — CO₂ ionizes at higher energy cost, and the dissociation chemistry adds parasitic losses — but the materials problem that has kept Earth-VLEO air-breathers grounded for a decade does not exist. You trade some isp — the exhaust velocity per unit propellant mass, which sets how much push you get per gram — for a thruster that survives. That is a trade-off we can accept.
Density profile. The relevant question is not "how much atmosphere is there" but "over what altitude range can you operate." Earth's thermosphere drops off steeply; the band where intake density is useful and drag is survivable is narrow, perhaps thirty kilometers wide, and the spacecraft must hold station inside it within a few kilometers of tolerance. Venus has a more extended atmosphere — the consequence of higher surface temperature and a different scale height — and the corresponding operational band is meaningfully broader. A control system that would struggle at Earth has margin at Venus. This is not a small point. Half the difficulty of any low-altitude orbital architecture is the closed loop between drag prediction, thrust modulation, and attitude control. Widening the corridor relaxes every one of those problems at once. And widens what kind of science can be done.
Solar power. A Hall thruster is power-limited. Thrust scales roughly with electrical input. Stand outside on the brightest summer afternoon in Connecticut and you receive about 1,000 watts per square meter through the haze. At Venus the spacecraft receives 2,600 watts per square meter of solar flux, against 1,360 at Earth — nearly double — and four and a half times as much as Mars. Half the array does the same job. Every other system on the spacecraft gets smaller. The smallest Earth-VLEO concepts struggle to close their power budgets; the Venus version has slack.
Magnetic field, or rather its absence. Earth's magnetosphere is a feature when you are sheltering biology and a problem when you are trying to keep a constant-attitude spacecraft pointed and stable in a low-altitude orbit, because the residual field interacts with currents in the bus to produce torques that must be dumped. Venus has effectively no intrinsic magnetic field. The induced magnetosphere from solar wind interaction is weak and confined to higher altitudes. For a spacecraft in the scooping band, the magnetic environment is, for all practical purposes, quiet as it is on Mars. This simplifies attitude control to the same degree that the wider density corridor simplifies orbital control.
These four factors do not compound equally. One that matters most; the rest are bonuses. But they all point the same direction, which is rare in mission architecture. Usually a destination is hostile in some respects and helpful in others, and the mission designer's job is to balance the ledger. I worked as an architect for several decades, and when four constraints all push in the same direction,  we are being given a gift. Venus's upper atmosphere is, for an air-breathing Hall thruster, helpful in every respect that matters.
The deeper point is that Venus has been catalogued for sixty years as the planet that punishes hardware. But the punishment is local. It is concentrated in the bottom fifty kilometers of the atmosphere, where the pressure, temperature, and sulfuric acid combine into a hell that the lower stratosphere of any other planet does not approach. Above that hell — above the cloud deck, above the haze layer, in the thin, cold, mostly-CO₂ exosphere where an air-breathing thruster wants to fly — Venus is the most cooperative destination in the inner solar system for the propulsion technology that I think will define the next fifty years. A planet's lower atmosphere is its harshest layer; its exosphere is, for the right thruster, its most cooperative — and the thruster lives in the upper atmosphere by choice. The same features that make Venus inhospitable to landers make it hospitable to RAM-EP.

II. What the mission actually looks like
Start with the bus. Fifty to a hundred kilograms, dominated by solar panels and a single Hall thruster of the Gauss class — the commodity module Rocket Lab now sells. Power runs at two to three kilowatts near Earth and doubles on arrival at Venus. The structure is an aluminum frame with the intake duct running through its long axis, a deployable sun shade on the inward side and radiators on the outward. The thermal problem at Venus is not as severe as the public imagination suggests; at orbital altitude you are outside the greenhouse and the spacecraft sees roughly the same heat load as a sun-facing satellite at Mercury distance — a solved problem.
The propellant tank is small. Not zero — the cruise from Earth to Venus is conventional, on stored xenon — but small. Call it three to five kilograms, enough for the heliocentric spiral and the initial Venus capture. After insertion, the excess reaction mass in the tank is essentially decorative but may have some interesting benefits for science. Certainly one might imagine a sample return mission. But afterward, all the reaction mass can come from the planet.
Cruise lasts nine to fourteen months depending on launch geometry and how aggressive you want to be with the trajectory. The heliocentric leg — the time spent spiraling between the two planetary orbits — is about five and a half months at Gauss-class thrust. The two planetary spirals, climbing out of Earth's gravity well and being captured by Venus's, add another three to eight months between them, depending on starting orbit and final periapsis. A Hohmann transfer, by comparison — the conventional chemical-burn trajectory between two coplanar orbits — makes the heliocentric leg in about 150 days but demands far more launch mass. If you want to see why, and what changes when you turn the thrust knob up or down, an interactive companion to this essay lets you drag the thrust slider yourself and watch the cruise time and revolution count update in real time. It opens in a browser, runs locally, and requires nothing installed. The point of the slider is not to do mission design — it is to give the reader a physical intuition for why low-thrust spirals take the time they do, and why the choice of thruster class sets the whole calendar of the mission. The default position, 0.30 mm/s² on a 100 kg spacecraft, is the Gauss-class working point. Drop the slider to a cubesat thruster and the cruise stretches past three years; raise it to Psyche levels and the spiral collapses toward a Hohmann arc. The whole economic argument for low-thrust to Venus lives in the middle of that slider.
Arrival could be by aerocapture. It requires a blunt aeroshell and a mechanism for jettisoning it. But simplest solution is propulsive insertion using the same stored xenon that powered the cruise. This is slower — weeks to months of perigee-lowering passes — but mechanically simpler. No aeroshell, no separation event, no thermal protection. You arrive in a high elliptic orbit and you spiral down. This is what VERITAS itself plans to do. For the cubesat version of the mission, with the bus already optimized for slow electric maneuvering, this option may actually be preferred. Aerocapture is the high-leverage move but it concentrates all the risk into a sixty-second flight regime. Slow spiral insertion distributes the risk across weeks of margin-rich operations. 
The mission opens for science when the spacecraft drops its perigee low enough that the intake starts producing useful mass flow. Call it 180 kilometers, plus or minus, depending on the solar cycle and atmospheric state. Below that altitude drag exceeds thrust; above it the intake starves. The spacecraft does not fly at one altitude but in a controlled descent-and-correct cycle, drifting down by a few kilometers per orbit and pulsing the thruster to recover. This is exactly the control problem that ESA's RAM-EP team has been simulating for Earth missions for a decade, and the Venus version is, as established, easier in every dimension that matters.
A second interactive companion lets the reader explore the altitude and science investigation tradeoff question directly. Six sliders — periapsis altitude, intake area, collection efficiency, exhaust velocity, solar panel area, bus drag area — drive a live trade chart, a verdict box, and an altitude-gated list of the science unlocked by each operating regime. The non-specialist reader who would never sit through a partial-derivative argument can learn the shape of the problem in five minutes of slider-dragging: that thrust-to-drag depends on the geometry of the intake, not on how deeply you dive; that flying lower buys you direct sampling of the unknown UV absorber but costs you electrical power faster than any reasonable array can supply it; that the operating window where all constraints close simultaneously is roughly twenty-five kilometers wide. 
From this point the spacecraft is propellant-free. There is no fuel gauge falling toward empty. The mission's lifetime is bounded by something else entirely — the radiation tolerance of the avionics, the gradual degradation of the solar arrays, the wear of the thruster discharge channel, the cumulative drift of the attitude control system. These are slow numbers. Modern radiation-hardened processors are good for a decade in Venus orbit; the ESA EnVision design assumes a four-year primary mission and a multi-year extension. The cubesat constellation version, with redundancy distributed across the swarm rather than internal to each spacecraft, can plausibly target a ten-year operational lifetime. That is a hundred times the duration of Venera. It is longer than Magellan, longer than Akatsuki, longer than any continuous Venus presence in human history.
The payload is where the constellation strategy starts to pay back its real dividend. A single Venus flagship has to carry every instrument it might need, because there will not be another flagship for a decade. A constellation does not. Each spacecraft carries one or two instruments, chosen for that spacecraft's orbit and lifetime. One spacecraft carries a UV spectrometer pointed at the unknown absorber that has haunted Venus science since the 1970s — the dark patches in cloud-top imagery that we still cannot identify chemically. Another carries radio occultation hardware paired with a sister spacecraft on the opposite hemisphere, so the pair can measure pressure and temperature profiles in slices through the upper atmosphere every few hours. A third carries a mass spectrometer optimized for trace species in the cloud deck — phosphine, if it is real; sulfur isotopes, which are not in doubt. Others carry experimental instruments that would never survive flagship-mission review boards: a lightning detector, a sprite imager, a polarimeter. The cost of failure on any one cubesat is the cost of that cubesat alone, which means the review board can say yes to questions that the flagship review board has to say no to.
We have enjoyed NASA flagships. The engineering is heroic. But every instrument has to fly on every mission because there will not be another launch for a decade. A constellation of cheap satellites would break this institutional history.
Once the per-spacecraft cost falls below the threshold where institutional review becomes proportionate to the risk, you stop building spacecraft the way NASA has built planetary missions since Mariner. You start building them the way universities build cubesats today: one team, one instrument, one good idea, one launch slot. Twenty of these reach Venus in the same decade. Half of them work. The half that works returns more science than any single flagship plausibly could.
A note on the launch. The entire constellation can ride on a few Electron launches to a high Earth orbit or a single Neutron launch to Geostationary Transfer Orbit. From there each spacecraft spirals out independently. They do not need to launch together. They do not need to arrive at the same time. The constellation is built temporally rather than spatially. The first arrival begins returning science in 2032 or 2033. The last arrival, on a slower trajectory or a later launch, joins the swarm in 2035. By the time the last spacecraft reaches Venus, the first has been operating for two years, and the operations team has learned things about flying air-breathing thrusters at Venus. The later arrivals fly smarter than the earlier ones because the operations are no longer experimental.
This is what a Venus mission looks like in 2030 and after. Not one billion-dollar flagship. Twenty or thirty small craft, each cheap enough that the loss of any one is recoverable, each operating for a decade on a planet that has never sustained a working spacecraft for more than a few hours. The technology that makes it possible is a Gauss Hall thruster and a slightly unusual intake duct.

III. The wear problem, and the architecture it implies
A Hall thruster fed by Venus's atmosphere does not run forever. It runs for a while, and then it wears out.
Inside the discharge channel, the annular ceramic chamber where ionization and acceleration occur, energetic ions strike the walls and sputter material away. The erosion rate in steady operation is a few micrometers per hour. The numbers are small but relentless. Over tens of thousands of hours the channel wears through, the underlying magnetic circuit is exposed to the plasma, and the thruster begins ejecting ferrous material into the spacecraft environment. That is the end of life. There is no graceful degradation. The thruster runs at near-spec; then it fails.
In the 2010s, JPL and Aerojet Rocketdyne demonstrated magnetic shielding, a technique that pushes the high-energy ion sheath away from the walls and onto a region where ablation does not matter. The wall erosion rate falls by a factor of ten to a hundred. The discharge channel, instead of wearing through in five thousand hours, lasts fifty thousand. Imagine starting an engine in your driveway the week your daughter is born and turning it off the week she finishes kindergarten. That is the qualified firing duration of one of the Hall thrusters now flying on NASA's Psyche spacecraft, en route to the metal asteroid as I write this. Note the word firing. Psyche coasts between burns; the 50,000 hours is time on, not time elapsed. A Venus air-breather has no coast phase. It fires continuously to hold station against drag, which means the calendar life of the unit equals its firing life. The constraint is harsher than Psyche's by an order of magnitude in wall-clock time. The same shielding technique has now been demonstrated at sub-kilowatt scale in JPL's MaSMi miniature thruster, which completed a 7,205-hour wear test in 2021 processing more than a hundred kilograms of xenon with no measurable performance degradation. The erosion problem, for xenon-fed Hall thrusters of the class that could fly to Venus, is solved.
But the qualifier in that sentence is "for xenon-fed." The Venus architecture does not run on xenon. It runs on whatever the upper atmosphere happens to deliver, which is mostly carbon dioxide with a side of atomic oxygen, hydrogen, and helium. A 2025 study from Georgia Tech operated a sub-500-watt Hall thruster on five different propellants including CO₂ and found no evidence of oxidation or carbon soot buildup after ten hours of continuous operation. Ten hours is encouraging. Ten hours is not seven thousand hours. Nobody has yet run the long test that would establish whether magnetic shielding's erosion benefits transfer cleanly from xenon to a propellant whose dissociation products include atomic oxygen. There are physics-based reasons to think they will — the shielding works by geometry, not by chemistry, and the ion energies at the wall are reduced regardless of what species is being accelerated. There are also reasons to worry, particularly about atomic oxygen liberated in the discharge attacking the magnetic pole faces that the shielded design exposes more directly than an unshielded thruster does. The honest answer is that the next decade of laboratory work will settle the question. The architecture I am describing depends on the answer being yes. I think it might be.
Suppose it can, but with a caveat: the CO₂-fed thruster lifetime is only ‘good’. Call it five to seven years of continuous operation, comparable to Psyche's xenon-fed qualification, before erosion ends the life of the unit. The bus around it — solar panels, electronics, instruments, structure — is rated for fifteen to twenty years in Venus orbit, probably limited by radiation. The mismatch matters. You are now flying spacecraft whose propulsion lifetime falls short of the science mission.
Three architectural responses are possible, and the choice between them is the most interesting design decision in the entire mission.
The first is to accept the mismatch. Five to seven years of science return per spacecraft is already an order of magnitude longer than any previous Venus mission. The constellation continues to operate as individual thrusters die, gradually losing altitude control. By year ten the swarm has thinned by half and by year fifteen it is gone. This is the path of least architectural complexity, and a reasonable place to start. You learn what works, and a second-generation constellation benefits.
The second is to replace a unified thruster module. A service vehicle, launched from Earth in the seventh year of the mission, arrives at Venus carrying a payload of fresh Gauss thrusters and rendezvouses with each member of the constellation in turn. This is probably not a simple engineering problem. For starters, there is no ground-station to provide information on the status of each satellite. A safe orbit around each is required to inspect it. The grapple and the swap of the propulsion module might be the simplest part. The economics might work if the cost of the service mission is less than the cost of launching a replacement constellation. For twenty spacecraft, each costing $30 million to build and deliver, the math says the service mission can spend up to half a billion dollars and still work. That is not a flagship budget, but perhaps a fundable one.
The third is the move that I think is correct and that I have not yet seen anyone propose in the popular literature. Most of a Hall thruster is indefinite-lived. The magnets are permanent magnets. The cathode, with appropriate care, runs for tens of thousands of hours. The power processing unit is solid-state and rated for the life of the bus. The part that wears is the discharge channel itself.
Picture the part. A ceramic ring the size of a coffee saucer, perhaps a centimeter thick, white or pale gray, smooth on the outside and grooved on the inside from the slow scour of plasma. It weighs less than a hardcover book. The thruster it lives inside weighs ten times as much. The spacecraft that carries the thruster weighs a thousand times as much. The mission that the spacecraft is part of cost a hundred million dollars and will run for fifteen years. The part that decides how long the mission lasts is just the ring.
Manual workers understand that part that wears, needs to be designed for replacement.  A plane blade is replaceable. When you are lucky, the wearing part is the one with the simplest geometry. Like a spark plug, not a car’s engine. And this ceramic ring is crying for a solution.
If the discharge channel is designed for in-orbit replacement — and there is no physics reason it could not be, only the historical accident that nobody has ever needed to replace one — then the constellation member spacecraft themselves can carry replacement inserts rather than waiting for a rendezvous with a service tug. The architectural question becomes whether the discharge channel can be made into a field-replaceable unit, and that is a mechanical-design problem of the same character as the toner cartridge or the printer drum: not trivial, not hard, mostly a matter of deciding to do it.
So twenty to thirty small spacecraft are launched on one or two launches between 2030 and 2033. They spiral out to Venus on stored xenon, capture into orbit, and descend to the scooping altitude where they begin running on local atmosphere. They dive deeper to get scientific data as required. They operate for five to seven years on their primary thrusters. In the seventh or eighth year a single small servicing tug — itself running a Hall thruster all the way out to Venus — arrives carrying enough replacement discharge channels or new Gauss units for the entire constellation, plus spares. This difficult operation takes perhaps a year of slow rendezvous and proximity operations. But when it finishes, the constellation has been re-lifed with the newest Gauss units, and the science mission continues into the 2040s. That might be too difficult, so a second generation constellation from earth with self-repair is builtin might be more practical.
The whole sequence — initial constellation, service mission, extended constellation — costs less, I suspect, than a single planetary flagship of the kind NASA has been launching since Mariner. The science return is not comparable: the constellation collects data continuously for fifteen to twenty years; a flagship collects for four. The flagship architecture, for Venus, becomes one chapter in a longer story.
The Venus mission of the 2030s is a different kind of object — a distributed, replaceable, self-fueling system whose lifetime is decoupled from its individual components. A slightly modified Hall thruster, a slightly cleverer mounting flange, and a ceramic ring the size of a coffee saucer. That is the design problem.

Conclusion
The Soviet engineers who built Venera 13 in the 1970s designed for survival. They knew their spacecraft would die quickly. The instrument package they delivered to the surface of Venus in 1982 transmitted for one hundred and twenty-seven minutes before the heat reached its electronics, and that was longer than anyone in the design team had dared to predict. They built around the limit, accepted the limit, and made the limit irrelevant by extracting more science per minute than any planetary mission before or since.
The next generation of Venus spacecraft is being built against a different limit. Not the temperature of the surface, which they will never touch. Not the chemistry of the cloud deck, which they will observe. The limit is the radiation tolerance of a modern processor, the gradual degradation of a solar array, the slow wear of a thruster discharge channel — limits measured in years and decades rather than minutes. The atmosphere that destroyed Venera could be the benefactor of its successors.
There is a kind of justice in this. The planet that punished the hardware most severely turned out to be carrying, in its upper atmosphere, the solution to the propellant problem that has constrained every electric-propulsion mission ever flown. The punishment was local, concentrated in the bottom fifty kilometers, in the hell that Venera engineers were forced to design against. Above that hell, the same planet is the most cooperative destination in the inner solar system for the propulsion technology that will define the next fifty years. We did not see this for sixty years because we were looking down. The technology that made us look up — the small thruster, the small spacecraft, the small budget — was not available until now.
A Hall thruster costs maybe fifty thousand dollars. A xenon fill costs nearly as much. At Venus, the second number goes to zero. When the propellant is free and the planet is closer than Mars and the mission lifetime is measured in decades rather than hours, the only remaining question is why we are not already there. That question has an answer: the technology only just arrived. The first spacecraft to take advantage of it could fly before the decade is out. It will not be a flagship. It will not be famous. It will be one of twenty or thirty small craft scattered across the upper atmosphere of a planet we used to call hell, and it will be there for ten years, and then twenty, and then as long as the electronics hold.
I suggested to Peter Beck, in the previous open letter, that I thought he owed his shareholders not fantasy but a future large enough to deserve their patience. This is one such future. It does not require any technology that does not exist. It does not require a flagship. It requires a few hundred Gauss thrusters, a few small launches, and the decision to do it. The engineer at Wallops I met in 1964 was correct about the propellant. He was wrong about the planet. That seems, in retrospect, a forgivable error.

How low can an air-breathing Venus orbiter fly?

Interactive trade study — a companion to “Venus, not Earth: Air-breathing satellites will fly first over Venus.”

The single most consequential decision in a Venus air-breathing mission is periapsis altitude. Lower altitudes open up more science — direct sampling of the cloud deck, in-situ chemistry of the unknown UV absorber, particle counts in the cryosphere — but at a cost. Drag rises sharply, the power required to outrun it rises faster, and at some point the satellite cannot sustain itself in orbit on intake propellant alone.

Drag the altitude slider and watch the trade move. The plot shows drag force and intake thrust as functions of altitude. The readout below the plot shows the four hard constraints: thrust margin, electrical power, heat load, and orbital decay rate. The science panel at the bottom lights up the capabilities your operating altitude unlocks.

This model is honest about its assumptions but does not pretend to be a flight tool. The qualitative shape of the trade is robust. Specific crossover altitudes are uncertain by perhaps ±15 km depending on solar cycle, intake geometry, and CO₂-Hall-thruster efficiency — three numbers no one will pin down without a flight test.

How to read this

The blue line is intake thrust; the red line is drag. Where the blue line is above the red line, the satellite can sustain orbit on local propellant alone. Where it falls below, the satellite is decelerating and needs stored propellant to recover — or it spirals down and re-enters.

But thrust beating drag is only the first constraint. The second is power: the discharge chamber needs roughly ½ ṁ v_ex² watts of jet power, divided by thruster efficiency, to actually accelerate the propellant. Below about 145 km the power demand explodes past what a reasonable solar array can deliver. The third constraint is heat — the dissipated kinetic energy of decelerated air becomes infrared radiation the spacecraft must shed.

The most interesting region is the band where all three constraints are simultaneously satisfied. With default settings that band is roughly 145 to 170 km. Below 145 the power budget breaks; above 170 the science access narrows quickly. The 25-kilometer window is where a Venus air-breathing mission lives.

Model assumptions

  • Density: Piecewise exponential fit to Pioneer Venus and Venus Express data. Anchor points: 1.4×10⁻⁹ kg/m³ at 140 km, 1.4×10⁻¹⁰ at 150 km, 7×10⁻¹² at 180 km. Real density varies ~3× between solar minimum and maximum.
  • Drag: D = ½ ρ v² C_d A_drag with C_d = 2.2 (free-molecular flow, fully accommodated).
  • Intake thrust: T = ρ v A_in η (v_ex − v). Net of the momentum the air already carries — the thruster has to add velocity, not start from rest.
  • Power: P_req = ½ ṁ v_ex² / η_thrust with η_thrust = 0.55 (typical Hall thruster). Available power = 2,600 W/m² × panel area × 30% cell efficiency.
  • Orbital velocity: Circular Keplerian: v = √(μ_V / (R_V + z)) with μ_V = 3.249×10¹⁴ m³/s², R_V = 6,052 km. About 7,240 m/s near 150 km altitude.
  • What is missing: Plane changes, eccentric orbits, drag-induced heating effects on intake efficiency, ionization losses, day/night density asymmetry (~3×), and altitude-dependent solar panel pointing constraints. Each of these is its own paper.

The Earth-to-Venus Spiral

Interactive companion to “Venus, not Earth: Air-breathing satellites will fly first over Venus.”

Drag the thrust slider below. Watch the cruise time and the number of revolutions change. The trade you are looking at is the entire economic case for low-thrust interplanetary missions: more thrust shortens the cruise but demands a bigger spacecraft and more propellant; less thrust means months of patience, but a tenfold improvement in delivered mass to Venus orbit.

The default position (0.30 mm/s² applied to a 100 kg spacecraft) corresponds to a single Rocket Lab Gauss-class Hall thruster — about 30 millinewtons of thrust, or roughly the weight of six paperclips. The slider extends down to a hypothetical micro-thruster and up to the larger Psyche-class units flying today.

Notice what changes as you move the slider. At very low thrust (around 0.05 mm/s²), the spacecraft makes four or more full revolutions before falling far enough to reach Venus, and the cruise stretches past three years. At the default Gauss-class thrust (0.30 mm/s² on a 100 kg bus, about 30 mN), you get a cruise of roughly five to six months and about half a revolution. This is the working point the essay describes. At higher thrust (above 0.6 mm/s²), the spiral collapses toward something resembling a Hohmann transfer arc.

The whole argument for low-thrust to Venus lives in that middle range. The spacecraft is small enough that the propellant fraction stays manageable, and the cruise is short enough that the operations team and the science team are still the same humans by the time the data starts coming back.

On the model. This is a planar, two-body, constant-thrust integration with the thrust vector aligned opposite to the velocity, which is the correct sense to spiral inward from Earth's orbit to Venus's. It does not include the orbital plane change for Venus's 3.4° inclination, the Earth-escape spiral, or the Venus-capture spiral; the heliocentric leg alone is what the slider controls. Real mission tools (GMAT, Copernicus) include all of these effects. None of them changes the qualitative shape of the spiral or the order-of-magnitude cruise time.

The Edelbaum approximation in the readout matches the numerical integration to within a few percent for low thrust (below ~0.15 mm/s²) and diverges progressively as thrust rises, because the spiral stops behaving like a slowly-changing circular orbit and starts behaving like a directed arc. This is the expected breakdown of the many-revolution assumption.
